Why UHP exists

The system
you actually
need.

Steven didn't start UHP to help people look better at the beach. He started it because every program he tried fell apart the moment a quarter got busy.

After working through the research — nutrition science, behavioural psychology, habit architecture — one thing became clear: the systems that work are the ones that require the least decision-making at the hardest moments.

The breaking point
Running a business. Letting everything else slide.
Long hours, high stress, meals skipped or grabbed from a petrol station. Energy was managed with caffeine. Every health goal started in January and was abandoned by March — not from lack of effort, but from programs that didn't fit real life.
The research phase
Building a framework that actually fits.
Two years studying with dietitians, performance coaches, and behavioural specialists. The goal was never a perfect plan — it was a simple, durable system that worked inside a demanding schedule, not despite it.
The proof
First clients. Consistent results.
Other founders started asking what had changed. Coaching began with a small group of business owners — and within 12 weeks, results were consistent: energy, body composition, focus, and performance, all improving together.

What we stand for

Four things
we never compromise.

Calendar-first design
Your system is built around your actual week — not an idealised version of it. Board meetings, client dinners, interstate travel, long days: all of it is accounted for before you even start.
Results, not routines
We track the KPIs that matter: energy, body composition, focus, sleep, compliance. You get honest data and honest feedback — not a cheerleader telling you you're doing great when you're not.
Simple systems, real outcomes
The best plan is the one you'll actually maintain. We cut everything that adds complexity without adding results — no macro apps, no 90-minute meal preps, no all-or-nothing thinking.
Accountability is the edge
Every business owner we've ever worked with already knew what they should be doing. The system and the person checking in on it every week — that's the variable that changes everything.
